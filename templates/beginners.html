Ways to Use Sanitary Products

Using Sanitary Pads:
Choose:
Select a pad size for your flow (pantyliner, regular, maxi).
Apply:
Stick the pad to your underwear by removing the adhesive film.
Change:
Replace every few hours or when soaked.
Dispose:
Wrap in toilet paper or its wrapper and throw in the trash.

Using Tampons:
Choose:
Select the lightest absorbency needed.
Insert:
Wash hands, relax, and use the applicator or finger to insert the tampon angled toward your tailbone.
Wear:
Change every 4-8 hours; donâ€™t exceed 8 hours to avoid TSS.
Remove:
Gently pull the string, wrap the tampon, and trash it.

Using Menstrual Cups:
Prepare:
Wash hands, fold the cup, and insert it toward your tailbone.
Wear:
Keep it in for 8-12 hours or until full.
Remove:
Gently pull the stem or rim, empty, and wash.
Reusable:
Clean and reinsert; disposable cups should be trashed, not flushed.

Recommended Diets During Period

Recommended Diets During Menstruation:
Hydration:
Drink 2.7 liters of water daily to combat dehydration, headaches, dizziness, and bloating.
Iron-Rich Foods:
Include fish, poultry, spinach, and collard greens to replenish iron lost during bleeding.
Protein and Fiber:
Foods high in protein and fiber help stabilize blood sugar, reduce cravings, and provide sustained energy.
Omega-3/Fish Oil:
Helps relieve cramps, depression, and mood swings.
Herbs (Ginger, Cinnamon):
Can reduce menstrual pain but consume in moderation.
Vitamins and Minerals:
Opt for nutrient-rich fruits, vegetables, and whole grains to boost overall well-being.

Foods to Avoid During Menstruation:
Salty/High-Sodium Foods:
Can cause water retention, bloating, and edema.
Sweets/High-Sugar Foods:
Lead to blood sugar fluctuations and mood swings.
Caffeine (Tea/Coffee):
May worsen water retention and headaches but can be consumed in moderation.
Alcohol:
Causes dehydration, bloating, and headaches.
Spicy Foods:
Can irritate the stomach, causing nausea, diarrhea, and discomfort.
Red Meat:
Rich in iron but high in prostaglandins, which may worsen cramps.

Exercise Tips During Menstruation

Exercises to Perform:
Light Walking or Cardio:
Opt for lower-intensity aerobic activities like walking or light cardio.Save high-intensity cardio for the end of your period when lung function improves.
Low-Volume Strength Training:
Utilize potential strength increases for low-volume strength and power-based workouts. Combine strength with light cardio for balanced sessions.
Yoga and Pilates:
Ideal during the days leading up to your period. Can help relieve cramps, fatigue, and soreness.
Continue Regular Workouts (If Comfortable): Stick to your normal routine if you feel fine, but reduce intensity if needed.

Exercises to Avoid:
High-Intensity Cardio or Endurance Training:
Avoid overexertion if you feel fatigued or notice a higher perceived exertion rate.
Skill and Precision-Based Training:
Skip exercises requiring high precision or technical skill during this time.
Excessive Training Stress:
Reduce training volume and stress while maintaining light activity to support your body.

Period Pain

Period pain is common and usually occurs around the start of a period or a few days before. It can affect anyone who menstruates and typically lasts up to 3 days, sometimes impacting daily activities.

Symptoms:
- Pain in the tummy before or at the start of your period.
- Cramps that may spread to your back and thighs.
- Sharp pain or a dull ache in the tummy.

Ways to Ease Period Pain:
- Take a warm bath or shower.
- Use a heat pad or hot water bottle wrapped in a tea towel on your tummy.
- Massage your tummy and back.
- Engage in gentle exercises like yoga, swimming, walking, or cycling.
- Take painkillers such as paracetamol or ibuprofen.
- Reduce alcohol consumption and avoid smoking.

Causes:
Period pain occurs due to the womb tightening during menstruation, which is a normal part of the menstrual cycle. However, it may also be caused by conditions such as:
- Endometriosis: Womb tissue growing in other areas.
- Adenomyosis: Womb tissue growing into the muscular wall of the womb.
- Fibroids: Growths in and around the womb.
- Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): Infection of the womb, fallopian tubes, or ovaries.

Unlock the Patterns of Your Cycle with Insights

✔ Answered by Dr. Barbara Levy

Vaginal discharge is a combination of bacteria, mucus, and fluid produced by the vagina and cervix that is shed through the vagina. It helps to keep the vagina clean and healthy, lubricates the vaginal tissues, and protects against infection.

Discharge is considered normal when it is clear, white, or slightly yellow without a strong odor or discomfort. If you notice changes like unusual color, foul smell, or irritation, it may be time to consult a healthcare provider.

Use a reliable period tracker app to log your cycle start date, end date, and symptoms. You can also take note of discharge patterns, mood changes, and other physical symptoms to predict your ovulation and period dates more accurately.

✔ Answered by BetterHealth

Talk to your doctor if you are worried about your periods. For example, if:
  • Your period patterns change
  • Your periods are getting heavier (i.e. you need to change your pad or tampon more often than every 2 hours)
  • Your periods last more than 8 days
  • Your periods come less than 21 days apart
  • Your periods come more than 2 to 3 months apart
  • Your symptoms are so painful they affect your quality of life
  • Your bleed between periods
  • Your bleed after sexual intercourse.

    • Premenstrual syndrome (PMS) is a series of symptoms that typically occur 1 to 2 weeks before the start of a period. Symptoms can include:
    • headache
    • fatigue
    • bloating
    • irritability
    PMS affects as many as 3 in 4 women, according to WomensHealth.gov.

    Menstruation has long been stigmatized, labeled "unclean" in historical texts. Myths like 1920’s "menotoxin" theory were later disproven.
    • 1897: First disposable pads (Lister’s Towels).
    • 1929: First tampon.
    • 1980s: Adhesive pads introduced.
    Today, products like menstrual cups and period apps focus on sustainability and breaking stigma.

    Despite being outlawed, chhaupadi—forcing women to isolate during menstruation—persists in rural Nepal. Women, supported by SNV and local leaders, are replacing sheds with private rooms, creating reusable pads, and raising awareness.
    • Menstrual health training for women and girls.
    • Educating men to support hygiene needs.
    • Installing water taps for better sanitation.
    These initiatives are reducing stigma, promoting health, and fostering equality.
